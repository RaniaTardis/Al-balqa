Jordanian Cuisine

Jordan is home to a rich culinary heritage, featuring a variety of traditional dishes enjoyed across the country. Experience the authentic flavors that have been passed down through generations, creating a delightful blend of textures and cultural history.

Main Dishes

  • Mansaf: Jordan's national dish featuring lamb served with rice and jameed (fermented dried yogurt), commonly prepared for celebrations and special occasions.
  • Makmoura: A beloved dish from northern Jordanian villages, consisting of layers of dough and seasoned chicken or meat baked together.
  • Kibbeh/Kebab: Popular across Jordan, especially in northern Irbid villages, made with bulgur wheat and spiced meat, often deep-fried or baked.
  • Maqluba: A hearty casserole of layered rice, vegetables, and meat, flipped upside down before serving—literally meaning "upside-down."
  • Musakhan: Roast chicken baked with caramelized onions, sumac, spices, and fried pine nuts, served on traditional taboon bread.
  • Meftoul: Large couscous-like wheat balls served with chickpeas and chicken cooked in aromatic broth.

Popular Dishes & Appetizers

  • Dafeen (Freekeh): A smoky, nutritious dish made with roasted green wheat, typically paired with meat or chicken.
  • Othan Al-Shayeb (Shish Barak): Dumplings stuffed with minced lamb, shaped like "ears," and cooked in yogurt-based sauce.
  • Qalayet Bandora: A simple yet essential dish of fried tomatoes, onions, olive oil, salt, and chili peppers, eaten with bread.
  • Falafel: Small deep-fried chickpea balls seasoned with Middle Eastern spices, known for their distinctive smaller size.
  • Hummus & Foul: Chickpea and fava bean-based dips commonly served for breakfast and as appetizers.
  • Chicken & Meat Trays: Roasted dishes served in large trays with vegetables and aromatic seasonings.

Traditional Desserts

  • Kunafa: The most famous Jordanian dessert, made with shredded phyllo pastry layered with cheese and drenched in sugar syrup.
  • Baklava: Delicate layers of phyllo pastry filled with chopped nuts and soaked in honey or syrup.
  • Qatayef: Sweet dumplings stuffed with cream or pistachios, traditionally eaten during Ramadan.
  • Lazagiyat: Soft crepe-like dessert layered with sheep butter and honey, made from milk and tahini dough.

Jordan's cuisine is a delightful blend of flavors, textures, and cultural history, making it an essential part of the country's identity!
